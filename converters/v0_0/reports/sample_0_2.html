Black Holes and the Schwarzschild Metric

1. Introduction

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape once it crosses the event horizon. The first exact solution to Einstein's field equations describing such an object was found by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916, just months after Einstein published the general theory of relativity.

2. The Schwarzschild Solution

2.1 The Metric

For a static, spherically symmetric, uncharged mass \(M\) in vacuum, the spacetime interval is

\[ds^2 = -\left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right)c^2\,dt^2 + \left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2\]

where \(d\Omega^2 = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, d\phi^2\) is the solid angle element, and the Schwarzschild radius is

\[r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}\]

At \(r = r_s\) the metric component \(g_{tt}\) vanishes and \(g_{rr}\) diverges — this is the event horizon. The singularity at \(r = 0\) is a genuine curvature singularity.

2.2 Gravitational Time Dilation

A clock at rest at coordinate radius \(r\) ticks slower than a clock at infinity. The ratio of proper time \(d\tau\) to coordinate time \(dt\) is

\[\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{r}}\]

As \(r \to r_s^+\), time dilation becomes infinite — a distant observer watches infalling matter freeze at the horizon, redshifting to invisibility.

2.3 Photon Sphere and ISCO

Two special radii govern the structure around a Schwarzschild black hole:

FeatureRadiusDescription
Event horizon\(r_s = 2GM/c^2\)point of no return
Photon sphere\(r_{\gamma} = \tfrac{3}{2}r_s\)unstable circular photon orbit
ISCO\(r_{\text{ISCO}} = 3r_s\)innermost stable circular orbit for massive particles

The ISCO sets the inner edge of accretion discs and is directly observable in X-ray binaries.

3. Hawking Radiation

3.1 Thermal Emission

Quantum field theory in curved spacetime predicts that black holes radiate thermally at the Hawking temperature

\[T_H = \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}\]

This is inversely proportional to mass: stellar-mass black holes (\(M \sim 10 M_\odot\)) have \(T_H \sim 10^{-8}\,\text{K}\), far below the CMB temperature, and evaporation timescales far exceed the age of the universe.

3.2 Evaporation Timescale

By Stefan–Boltzmann radiation and energy conservation, the evaporation time scales as

\[t_{\text{evap}} \sim \frac{5120\, \pi\, G^2 M^3}{\hbar\, c^4}\]

For a primordial black hole to be evaporating today (\(t_{\text{evap}} \approx 13.8\,\text{Gyr}\)), its initial mass must have been \(M \approx 5 \times 10^{11}\,\text{kg}\) — about the mass of a mountain.

4. Numerical Exploration

4.1 Schwarzschild Radii of Familiar Objects

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

G = 6.674e-11
c = 2.998e8
hbar = 1.055e-34
k_B = 1.381e-23

objects = {
    'Earth':      5.972e24,
    'Sun':        1.989e30,
    'Stellar BH (10 Msun)': 10 * 1.989e30,
    'Sgr A* (4e6 Msun)':    4e6 * 1.989e30,
    'M87* (6.5e9 Msun)':    6.5e9 * 1.989e30,
}

print(f'{'Object':<30} {'Mass (kg)':>15} {'r_s (m)':>15} {'T_H (K)':>15}')
print('-' * 80)
for name, M in objects.items():
    rs = 2 * G * M / c**2
    TH = hbar * c**3 / (8 * np.pi * G * M * k_B)
    print(f'{name:<30} {M:>15.3e} {rs:>15.3e} {TH:>15.3e}')

4.2 Time Dilation Profile

r_over_rs = np.linspace(1.01, 10, 1000)
dilation = np.sqrt(1 - 1 / r_over_rs)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(r_over_rs, dilation, linewidth=2)
ax.axvline(1.5, color='gray', linestyle='--', label='photon sphere ($1.5\,r_s$)')
ax.axvline(3.0, color='orange', linestyle='--', label='ISCO ($3\,r_s$)')
ax.set_xlabel('$r / r_s$')
ax.set_ylabel('$d\\tau / dt$')
ax.set_title('Gravitational Time Dilation near a Schwarzschild Black Hole')
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
