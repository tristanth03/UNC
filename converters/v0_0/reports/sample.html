Convergence of Fourier Series

1. Introduction

The Fourier series is a decomposition of a periodic function \(f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}\) into a sum of sinusoids. It is one of the most powerfull tools in classical analysis.

2. Theory

2.1 Definition

Definition

Let \(f \in L^2([-\pi, \pi])\). The Fourier series of \(f\) is

\[f(x) \sim \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(n)\, e^{inx}\]

where the Fourier coefficients are given by

\[\hat{f}(n) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\, e^{-inx}\, dx, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}\]

2.2 Convergence

2.2.1 $L^2$ Convergence

Theorem

For any \(f \in L^2([-\pi,\pi])\), the partial sums \(S_N f(x) = \sum_{|n| \leq N} \hat{f}(n) e^{inx}\) satisfy

\[\lim_{N \to \infty} \| S_N f - f \|_{L^2} = 0\]

and Parseval's identity holds:

\[\| f \|_{L^2}^2 = 2\pi \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} |\hat{f}(n)|^2\]

2.2.2 Pointwise Convergence

Proposition

If \(f\) is continuously differentiable at \(x_0\), then \(S_N f(x_0) \to f(x_0)\) as \(N \to \infty\). More precisely, the Dirichlet kernel \(D_N\) satisfies

\[S_N f(x) = (f * D_N)(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x - t)\, D_N(t)\, dt\]

where

\[D_N(t) = \sum_{n=-N}^{N} e^{int} = \frac{\sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})t\bigr)}{\sin(t/2)}\]
2.2.2.1 Gibbs Phenomenon
Remark

At a jump discontinuity of \(f\), the partial sums overshoot by approximately \(9\%\) regardless of \(N\). Precisely, if \(f\) has a jump of size \(\delta = f(x_0^+) - f(x_0^-)\), then

\[\limsup_{N\to\infty} S_N f(x) \approx f(x_0^+) + 0.0895\, \delta\]

3.2 Decay of Coefficients

Proposition

For the square wave \(f(x) = \mathrm{sgn}(x)\), the Fourier coefficients satisfy \(\hat{f}(n) = 0\) for even \(n\), and

\[\hat{f}(n) = \frac{2}{i\pi n}, \quad n \text{ odd}\]

so \(|\hat{f}(n)| = O(n^{-1})\), consistent with the \(C^0 \setminus C^1\) regularity of \(f\). In general, \(k\) derivatives in \(L^2\) implies \(|\hat{f}(n)| = O(n^{-k})\).
